# Purpose

Jeevamrutha is a **microbial inoculant**, not a fertilizer.

Its purpose is to multiply beneficial microorganisms and introduce them into the soil. These microorganisms continuously convert unavailable nutrients already present in the soil into plant-available forms, improving soil biological activity and supporting healthy crop growth.

---

# Core Philosophy

According to Subhash Palekar's Natural Farming philosophy:

- Soil already contains most of the nutrients required for crop growth.
- Healthy microorganisms unlock these nutrients.
- Indigenous cow dung and urine are used primarily as microbial inoculants rather than nutrient sources.
- Building soil biology is more important than supplying external fertilizers.

> **Note:** Statements such as "one indigenous cow can support 30 acres" are part of Palekar's natural farming philosophy and field practice. They should not be interpreted as universally validated scientific claims.

---

# Why Indigenous Cow Dung?

The emphasis is on the microorganisms present in the dung, not on the manure itself.

Instead of using dung as bulk fertilizer, Jeevamrutha multiplies these microorganisms so they can colonize the soil and continue nutrient cycling.

---

# Ingredients (200 L Batch)

| Ingredient | Quantity | Purpose |
|------------|----------|---------|
| Water | 200 L | Medium for microbial growth |
| Indigenous cow dung | 5–10 kg | Source of beneficial microorganisms |
| Indigenous cow urine | 5–10 L | Supports microbial multiplication |
| Pulse flour (non-oilseed) | 2 kg | Nitrogen and protein source for microbes |
| Jaggery | 2 kg | Energy source for microbial growth |
| Healthy chemical-free soil | 1 handful | Introduces diverse native microorganisms |

### Suitable Pulse Flour

Examples:

- Green gram
- Red gram
- Horse gram

Avoid:

- Groundnut
- Soybean
- Other oil-rich seeds

### Jaggery

Prefer:

- Dark jaggery
- Minimally processed jaggery

---

# Preparation Procedure

1. Fill a clean 200 L drum with water.
2. Add indigenous cow dung.
3. Add indigenous cow urine.
4. Add pulse flour.
5. Add jaggery.
6. Add one handful of healthy chemical-free soil.
7. Mix thoroughly.
8. Stir clockwise (or consistently in one direction) two to three times each day.
9. Allow fermentation.

---

# Fermentation

Minimum usable period:

- 2 days

Best usage period:

- Within 7 days

During cooler weather:

- Up to 10 days

Avoid storing for extended periods.

---

# Application Methods

Jeevamrutha can be applied through:

- Drip irrigation (after filtering)
- Sprinkler irrigation
- Soil drenching
- Direct field application

According to the speaker, Jeevamrutha is generally safe for crops when prepared and applied correctly.

---

# Recommended Frequency

## Short-duration crops

Apply approximately twice during a two-month crop cycle.

---

## Long-duration crops

Examples:

- Sugarcane
- Banana
- Coconut
- Arecanut
- Fruit orchards

Apply once every month.

# Important Principle

Jeevamrutha alone is not sufficient.

Successful natural farming also depends on:

- Crop diversity
- Continuous living roots
- Mulching
- Biological nitrogen fixation
- Intercropping
- Healthy soil biology

---

# Nitrogen Philosophy

Most nitrogen exists in the atmosphere.

Leguminous crops help make atmospheric nitrogen available through their association with nitrogen-fixing microorganisms.

Therefore, combine monocots and legumes whenever possible.

Examples:

- Maize + Red gram
- Sugarcane + Chickpea
- Millets + Pulses

---

# Recommended Nitrogen-Fixing Plants

The transcript recommends integrating plants such as:

- Drumstick (Moringa)
- Agase
- Gliricidia
- Other green manure trees

These contribute to long-term soil fertility.

---

# Intercropping Principle

Follow:

- Monocot + Dicot
- Dicot + Monocot

Examples:

- Sugarcane + Chickpea
- Sugarcane + Groundnut
- Maize + Tur
- Fruit orchard + Legumes

Benefits:

- Improved nutrient cycling
- Biological nitrogen fixation
- Greater biodiversity
- Reduced pest pressure
- Additional farm income

---

# Crop Rotation

Avoid continuous monocropping.

Examples suggested:

After onion:

- Groundnut
- Chickpea

Crop rotation improves soil biology and reduces pest and disease buildup.

# Key Takeaways

1. Jeevamrutha is a microbial inoculant rather than a fertilizer.
2. Indigenous cow dung serves as a source of beneficial microorganisms.
3. Healthy soil biology is the foundation of crop nutrition.
4. Regular application is more important than excessive application.
5. Intercrop monocots with legumes whenever practical.
6. Rotate crops to improve soil health.
7. Integrate nitrogen-fixing plants into the farming system.
8. Share knowledge and experiences with other farmers.
9. Focus on soil life rather than external chemical inputs.
10. The long-term goal is profitable, regenerative, chemical-free farming.
